Collect Images. Go to the Library.

After taking a walk through Thinking Machines, I went back up to the library to visit with Jenny Tobias to follow up with a question I had earlier. I was curious about art that sat at the intersection of vector graphics and photography.

Hours later, I was neck deep in art I couldn't have possibly imagined, much less Googled… Jenny had pulled literally dozens of works, notably: original(!) Bauhaus magazines, deep Kurt Schwitterz and Andy Warhol cuts, and modern digital art books such as Stress Press by Anouk Kruithof, Dutch Landscapes by Mishka Henner, and Library of the Printed Web by Paul Soulellis.

Although I was blown away by the breadth of material, I still didn't feel like I had found quite what I was looking for, and Jenny could tell from my reactions. The works were all just a bit left or right of that graphical-photographical sweet spot I was after. Struggling to find words to refine our search, I resorted to taking out my laptop and pulling up some images from a folder I've been adding to for the last few years.

After going through those images, even though she said that what I'm looking for doesn't quite fit a category, Jenny (with the help of a colleague) pointed me to some incredible, inspirational works:

  • Irving Penn's frozen food photography
  • Lorenzo Vitturi's Dalston Anatomy
  • Michael Najjar's High Altitude

These three pieces, not only showed me what I was looking for, but also recontextualized my initial question. Sure, visually I was looking for graphical-photographical interaction, but I was actually curious about the philosophical gray area between digital and analog, where art can be surprising and playful, yet deeply compelling. And these works absolutely exemplify that idea.

Only through conversation with the extremely knowledgable and helpful Jenny Tobias and staff at the MoMA Archives and Library could I have figured that out.
